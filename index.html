Format

What is the CCAT and how is it structured?

The Criteria Cognitive Aptitude Test (CCAT) is a pre-employment aptitude test designed to measure problem-solving, critical thinking, and the ability to learn new information quickly.

Key facts

  • 50 questions total.
  • 15 minutes to answer as many as you can.
  • Questions span numerical, verbal, and abstract/logical reasoning.
  • You're not expected to finish — accuracy and pace both matter.
  • No calculator allowed; mental math and estimation are key.

Your score reflects both how many questions you attempt and how many you get right. There is no penalty for wrong answers — guessing is always better than leaving blanks.

Score ranges & percentiles

Approximate score benchmarks (not official cutoffs — each employer sets their own):

  • Below 20 – below average; focus on pacing and fundamentals.
  • 20–29 – average; solid for many roles.
  • 30–39 – strong; competitive for analytical positions.
  • 40+ – excellent; typically top percentiles.

Your goal here is to get comfortable with the pace and question types so your score reflects your true ability.

Strategy

Tips & tricks for a higher CCAT score

The biggest challenge on the CCAT isn't any single question — it's the time pressure. Here are practical tactics you can apply immediately.

1. Don't get stuck

If a question is taking more than ~30–40 seconds and you're still lost, guess and move on. The opportunity cost of spending 2–3 minutes on one problem is huge.

2. Guess intelligently

There's no negative marking. Eliminate obviously wrong options and guess from the remaining ones. Two options left? You've turned it into a 50-50.

3. Use rough math

Most questions don't require perfect arithmetic. Estimation, simplifying fractions, and recognizing common percent values (10%, 25%, 50%) will save time.

4. Spot patterns fast

For sequences and logic, look first at simple differences, ratios, or "×2 + 1" style patterns before considering more complex ideas.

5. Manage anxiety

The countdown clock can be intimidating. That's why we include a timed free simulation — so the real CCAT feels like "just another practice run" you've done before.

6. Practice under real conditions

Sitting with a timer, one question at a time, and no ability to go back is very different from casually scrolling a question bank. Use the free simulation to rehearse the real thing.

Examples

Example CCAT-style questions with answers

A few representative questions across numerical, verbal, and logical reasoning — in the same spirit as what you'll see in the actual exam.

Numerical reasoning
A factory produces 210 units in 7 hours at a constant rate. How many units does it produce per hour?
Answer: 30 units/hour. Compute 210 ÷ 7 = 30.
Verbal reasoning
Choose the word most similar in meaning to "concise".
Answer: Brief. "Concise" means using few words while still being clear.
Logical / pattern
What comes next in the sequence: 3, 7, 15, 31, ?
Answer: 63. Pattern is "double and add 1": 3→7, 7→15, 15→31, so 31×2+1 = 63.
